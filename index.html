Ahmedabad Plane Crash: Fuel Cutoff Mystery at Heart of Preliminary Report

Ahmedabad Plane Crash: What Really Happened in the Cockpit?
By Naitik Sharma

A wave of shock swept through the nation when a domestic passenger aircraft crashed during its final approach to Ahmedabad airport last week. Initial speculation pointed toward technical malfunction, sudden weather turbulence, or even a possible bird strike. Social media was flooded with questions—and conspiracy theories. But now, the Government of India has released its preliminary investigation report, shedding light on what truly went wrong.

According to the Directorate General of Civil Aviation (DGCA), the crash was caused by human error compounded by miscommunication in the cockpit. The black box recording reveals a tense final minute between the pilot and co-pilot. The aircraft had been cleared for landing on Runway 23, but visibility dropped sharply due to unexpected crosswinds. The pilot reportedly said, “I think we should go around,” to which the co-pilot replied, “We’re stable, let's proceed.” That moment of indecision, lasting barely four seconds, sealed the fate of the flight.

The report indicates that there was no mechanical failure, and all systems were functioning normally until impact. The confusion stemmed from a breakdown in Crew Resource Management (CRM), which is crucial during such high-pressure moments. Eyewitnesses from nearby areas recalled hearing a loud thud followed by plumes of smoke. Emergency services responded within minutes, saving many lives despite the tragic loss of a few passengers and crew members.

The government has now issued strict directives to all domestic airlines, emphasizing simulator-based crosswind training and mandatory refresher courses on cockpit coordination. As investigations continue, one thing is clear: this was not a machine failure, but a human mistake made under pressure—a painful reminder of how every second matters in aviation.
